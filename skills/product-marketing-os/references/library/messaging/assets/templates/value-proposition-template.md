# Value Proposition Template (Pain-Claim-Gain)

**Purpose:** Turn positioning into individual value propositions using Pain-Claim-Gain: the customer's problem (Pain), what the product does about it (Claim), and the measurable outcome (Gain). The customer is the hero; the product is the tool.

**When to use:** After the [messaging canvas](messaging-canvas-template.md) defines pillars, when you need the specific sentences — homepage sections, ad copy, one-liners, email hooks. Also standalone, when someone asks "what's the value prop for [persona]?" and you catch yourself answering with a feature list.

**Time to complete:** 30-60 minutes per value proposition, including sourcing the pain verbatim.

## Instructions

1. Start from a real pain, in the customer's words. If you can't quote it, you haven't researched it — a paraphrased pain produces a generic prop.
2. Classify the pain (process / performance / cost / risk / growth). The type tells you which gain will land: process pains want time back, risk pains want certainty.
3. Write the claim with a strong verb and page-level specificity. "Provides visibility" is dead on arrival; "answers in four minutes, query shown" survives.
4. Make the gain measurable and give it a comparator. A number with nothing to compare against is trivia.
5. Compress each prop to the four-part line: **claim · who · proof · pain removed**. If any part is missing, the prop isn't done.
6. Run the competitor-swap test: put a rival's name in your claim. If the sentence still works, it's not a value proposition — it's a category description.

---

## The Structure

### Pain — what the customer struggles with (their words, not yours)

**Pain statement:** "[Verbatim or near-verbatim customer language]" — [source, date]

**Pain type:** [Process / Performance / Cost / Risk / Growth]

**Who feels it most:** [Persona — specific enough to almost call by name]

**What it costs them today:** [Quantified where possible — hours, money, missed outcomes, emotional toll]

### Claim — what the product does about it

**Claim:** [Action verb + specific capability + where/when it happens]

**Unique because:** [Why alternatives can't say this — trace to the positioning canvas's unique capabilities]

**Provable by:** [Demo / metric / customer quote — the proof you'd show a skeptic]

### Gain — the outcome the customer achieves

**Gain:** [Measurable outcome + comparator ("vs. what they do today")]

**Emotional payoff:** [What it *feels* like — relief, confidence, control; B2B buyers are still humans]

### The compressed line (claim · who · proof · pain removed)

> **"[Claim as a headline]"** · [who it's for] · [the one proof] · removes [the pain, named].

## Filled example — Plotline (three value propositions)

*Research base: Plotline launch corpus, desks dated 2026-06-12. Note prop 1's honesty flag — that discipline is part of the template.*

### Value proposition 1 — the wedge

**Pain:** "I know exactly what I want to know. I just can't write the query, so I file a ticket and wait." — PM, beta interview #7 (2026-06-04). The queue runs ~2 days ("Every 'why did this metric move' becomes a ticket… by the time it comes back, the sprint's planned" — growth PM, win/loss call), so decisions wait — or get made on gut and relitigated next week.
- **Type:** Process pain (the answer path runs through a queue) riding a Performance pain (fewer informed decisions per week).
- **Who:** PMs and growth owners at 20–200-person B2B SaaS with no (or overloaded) data team.
- **Cost today:** ~2-day median wait per question — and an unknown number of questions never asked because the queue made asking not worth it. *(estimate — nobody measures the unasked questions; that's the point.)*
- **Claim:** Plotline answers your plain-English question — "where do trial users drop off before inviting a teammate?" — in minutes, with the generated query and metric definition shown under the answer.
- **Unique because:** neither fetched rival ships plain-English self-serve — Vista is an analyst-first suite, Pulse is dashboards you still file a ticket to explain (homepage fetches, 2026-06-12). ⚠️ *"Only" stays internal until the rival AI-feature sweep closes; Pulse's Insights AI docs page 404'd.*
- **Provable by:** the live demo — type the question, the funnel comes back, the query is visible.

**Gain:** a 4-minute median time to answer vs. the ~2-day queue — the decision ships in the same sprint as the question.
- **Emotional payoff:** relief and self-sufficiency. The product speaks the ICP's own phrase back to them: no more "file a ticket and wait."

> **"Ask your product a question in plain English — get the answer in four minutes."** · PMs without a data team to lean on · the live ask-to-answer demo · removes the ticket-and-wait between question and decision.

### Value proposition 2 — the whole-team memory of what numbers mean

**Pain:** "We spent half the QBR arguing about whose activation number was right." — lifecycle marketer, win/loss call (2026-05-28). Every team's dashboard defines "activated" differently, so the meeting is about the numbers instead of the decision.
- **Type:** Risk pain (nobody trusts the numbers) riding a Process pain (reconciliation work before every review).
- **Who:** lifecycle/PMM measuring campaigns and activation; any org where product and marketing report the same metric differently.
- **Cost today:** recurring reconciliation arguments and decisions deferred to "let's align on the data first." *(assumption — the cost is attested but unquantified; a customer quote with a number is the named gap.)*
- **Claim:** Plotline's shared metric catalog defines each metric once — and every answer, for every team, uses that definition and shows it.
- **Unique because:** rivals leave definitions to whoever built each dashboard; catalog-enforced definitions in every self-serve answer is the depth no fetched alternative ships.
- **Provable by:** the same question asked by two teams returning the same number, definition visible (product demo).

**Gain:** numbers that reconcile by construction — vs. today, where every review starts with an argument about whose export is right.
- **Emotional payoff:** trust — the meeting is about what to do, not whose number to believe.

> **"One definition of 'activated' — every team, every answer."** · lifecycle and PMM teams · catalog-enforced definitions shown under each answer · removes the whose-number-is-right argument.

### Value proposition 3 — the suite people actually open

**Pain:** "We pay for Vista. Three people have logged in this quarter." — Head of Product, churned-Vista prospect call (2026-06-02). Powerful analytics bought for the whole team becomes shelfware, and the proposed fix is a data hire.
- **Type:** Cost pain (paying for shelfware + a pending hire) riding a Growth pain (the team can't scale its decision-making).
- **Who:** Heads of Product at 20–200-person B2B SaaS — the economic buyer.
- **Cost today:** a five-figure suite at single-digit adoption, plus the loaded cost of the data hire being floated to make it usable.
- **Claim:** Plotline is the analytics the whole team opens — anyone can ask, seats are unlimited, and pricing scales with usage (MTU), not headcount.
- **Unique because:** rivals monetize seats or platform fees, which taxes exactly the cross-team adoption the buyer wants; usage-based MTU pricing with free seats inverts it.
- **Provable by:** beta adoption telemetry — 71% of weekly active users write no SQL; the tool spreads past the two people who can query.

**Gain:** more decisions per week on trusted numbers — vs. shelfware plus a data-hire request.
- **Emotional payoff:** vindication — the analytics budget finally produces usage, not guilt.

> **"The analytics your whole team actually opens."** · Heads of Product tired of shelfware · 71% of active users write no SQL · removes the shelfware-plus-data-hire tax.
