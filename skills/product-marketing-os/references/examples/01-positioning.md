# Positioning — Plotline *(gold-standard example)*

*Sets the bar for `pmm-positioning-exercise` and `pmm-positioning-audit`. April
Dunford's components, each reasoned — not a tagline. Numbers/competitors are
illustrative.*

## 1. Competitive alternatives — what people use if Plotline doesn't exist

Not "other analytics tools." The honest set, by what the buyer actually does:

- **Vista Analytics (enterprise suite).** Powerful, but assumes a data person to
  instrument and maintain it. In our segment it becomes shelfware — bought,
  half-set-up, abandoned.
- **Pulse (mid-market point tool).** Easier than Vista, but still
  event-and-dashboard shaped: you can see *what* happened, then you file a ticket
  to ask *why*.
- **A SQL analyst + the data queue.** Accurate, but slow and rationed — a "quick
  question" is a multi-day ticket, so most questions never get asked.
- **Spreadsheets / gut (do-nothing).** Free and instant, but wrong often enough
  that the team relitigates the same decisions.

## 2. Unique attributes — what only Plotline has

1. **Plain-English questions → real analyses.** Ask "where do trial users drop
   off?" and get a funnel, no SQL. The others need a query or a pre-built report.
2. **Auto-instrumentation.** Events are captured without engineering tickets, so
   non-technical owners aren't blocked on a sprint to add tracking.
3. **Shared metric definitions.** One definition of "active," "activated," "MTU,"
   used across product and marketing — so numbers reconcile across teams.

## 3. Value — what those attributes let the customer do

- **The person with the question gets the answer themselves, in minutes** (no
  queue). That's the core unlock: decisions stop waiting on the data team.
- **Tools stop becoming shelfware** because adoption doesn't depend on a
  maintainer.
- **The org stops arguing about whose number is right** and argues about what to
  do — faster, calmer decisions.

## 4. Best-fit customers — who gets the most value

20–200-person B2B SaaS with real usage but **no (or overloaded) data team**, where
the PM/growth/PMM owner is currently blocked on a queue. Value is highest exactly
where the gap between "who needs the answer" and "who can get it" is widest.

## 5. Market category — the frame that makes the value obvious

**Product analytics**, positioned as *"self-serve product analytics — answers
without SQL."* We deliberately do **not** frame as BI (invites a warehouse/modeling
comparison we'd lose) or web analytics (wrong job). The category cue tells the
buyer "this is the one your PMs can actually run."

## Where we do NOT win (state it — it makes the rest credible)

- **Data-mature orgs** that want warehouse-native modeling: that's dbt + a BI
  tool, not us. Don't chase it.
- **On price vs. free web analytics** (GA): we're not free and shouldn't pretend
  the job is the same.
- **On raw analytical depth vs. Vista** for a dedicated analyst: we win on
  *who can use it*, not on the deepest possible query.

## One-line positioning statement

> For product and growth teams at scaling B2B SaaS **without a data team to lean
> on**, **Plotline** is the **self-serve product analytics tool** that answers
> "why" in plain English in minutes — unlike Vista or a SQL queue, which make the
> people with the questions wait on the few people who can write the query.

## Proof requirements (to make this defensible)

- "Answers in minutes" → the 4-min median time-to-first-answer stat + a live
  no-SQL demo. **Have.**
- "Stops being shelfware" → a retention/active-usage stat vs. category benchmark.
  **Partial** — have internal active-usage, need a comparative source.
- "Best-fit = no data team" → a named customer in that profile with an outcome.
  **Gap** — flag a case study.
